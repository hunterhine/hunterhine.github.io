University of Maryland crime reports dropped during COVID, but crime stats aren’t quite what they seem

By Hunter Hine

UMPD prepares for statistical changes, and University of Maryland criminology professors discuss the problems with representing crime through statistics

May 16, 2022

By Hunter Hine

Since the 2021 Fall semester began, reports of larceny theft at University of Maryland have risen to more closely resemble pre-pandemic trends, according to University of Maryland Police Department's crime reporting.

However, reported crime statistics don't always accurately portray the full extent of crime, according to two University of Maryland criminology professors.

Photograph by Hunter Hine
The UMPD is located in the Pocomoke building on Baltimore Avenue. The department has over 100 employees, according to the UMPD website.

The UMPD currently submits its crime data to the FBI through the Uniform Crime Report Summary Reporting System, or SRS, said UMPD spokesperson Sgt. Rosanne Hoaas.

The SRS tracks eight types of crime called index crimes, according to University of Maryland Criminology and Criminal Justice Research Professor James Lynch. Lynch previously served as director of the Bureau of Justice Statistics.

However, this year, the UMPD received its certification for the National Incident-Based Reporting System, or NIBRS, Hoaas said. NIBRS is a newer data system that collects more detail for each reported crime, and unlike the SRS, it can classify a single incident with several separate offensenses, according to the FBI website.

The UMPD is currently transitioning from the SRS to NIBRS, Hoaas said.

“Once we are fully ready to go we will make some form of public announcements or community notes because it's going to change the way that the recording looks,” Hoaas said.

The effects of the pandemic on crime and crime reporting are visible in the UMPD UCR’s statistics, Hoaas said.

In any given year, larceny theft is the most commonly reported crime at University of Maryland. In April of 2020, larceny theft reporting dropped to just 2 reports, compared to 17 reports in April 2019, according to the UMPD UCR. The drop is mostly attributable to the pandemic because there wasn’t a full campus, Hoaas said. University of Maryland transitioned to online school in April 2020 because of COVID-19.

In 2019, University of Maryland’s theft reports totaled at 243, according to the UCR. In 2020 they totalled 152, and in 2021 the total was 147. That’s a nearly 40% drop in larceny reports between 2019 and 2021. By September 2021, when students began returning to in person classes, reports of larceny increased.

“The initial stages COVID, people locked up. That had a real effect. All the opportunity goes away. Your only opportunity moves inside, into your households of domestic violence,” Lynch said.

But, there is a difference between how much crime truly occurs, and how much is reported to police, Lynch said.

The 2020 BJS National Crime Victimization Survey, or NCVS, found that only about 40% of violent victimizations and only about 33% of property victimizations were reported to police, according to the BJS.

Greg Midgette, an assistant professor in the University of Maryland’s Department of Criminology and Criminal Justice said that since the crime victimization survey is anonymous, it's expected that victims’ reporting bias is mitigated, giving a better look into true crime levels.

Many factors influence whether someone will report a crime, including the stigma of being a victim of certain crimes and whether or not a victim finds law enforcement trustworthy or reliable, Midgette said. It also depends on individual characteristics, such as age, socio-economic status, and whether or not the victim has a relationship with the perpetrator, Midgette said.

Midgette said that policing a university comes with unique factors that might affect crime reporting. Students and faculty at University of Maryland come from all over and bring different perspectives on crime and police, so their inclination to report a crime might vary greatly based on their past experience and beliefs, Midgette said.

“The police data is not going to be really good for minor assaults, for rapes, sexual assaults, typically, because citizens don't report them for a lot of complicated reasons,” Lynch said.

University of Maryland had about 41,271 students in the Fall 2021 semester, according to IRIS, and about 14,135 employees, including faculty, staff and graduate assistants, according to a Fall 2021 employee summary.

Lynch said he believes university police at large might face pressure to downplay crime.

“Some universities will get pressure. That doesn't mean they'll give into the pressure. They'll get pressure to downplay crime because it's bad for business,” Lynch said.

Lynch did not reference the UMPD specifically, nor did he suggest the university’s police doctor their statistics. Lynch praised the transparency of the UMPD.

“For us, if anything, we write more reports than you would find in a traditional Police Department, period. We are documenting things. You know, if you ask another police department, they would say no, we don't write reports for that, but we do,” Hoaas said.
